Figure Skating Jump Video Classification

By: Elena Golubeva and Vladislav Goteiner

Videos can be understood as a series of individual images; and therefore, many deep learning practitioners would be quick to treat video classification as performing image classification a total of N times, where N is the total number of frames in a video. Taking this into consideration, naive video classification method would be: loop over all frames in the video file; for each frame, pass the frame through the CNN (e.g. Resnet50 in our case, which is a convolutional neural network that is 50 layers deep); classify each frame individually and independently of each other; obtain the predictions from the CNN; compute the average of the last K predictions and choose the label with the largest corresponding probability; label the frame and write the output to disk. We demonstrate effectiveness of the proposed method with additional layers by applying it to the action recognition tasks on RGB videos. Experiments show that amount of frames and the addition of dense layers improves the results of the method.

The MURA challenge

By: Alon Avrahami, David Chernin, and Yair Hanina

The motivation of this paper is to determine whether a radiographic study is normal or abnormal, using convolution neural networks. This is a critical radiological task, a study interpreted as normal rules out disease and can eliminate the need for patients to undergo further diagnostic procedures or interventions. This study is very common in the AI industry, also, this study is related to health care, which is very important to humanity. These reasons motivate the use of a flexible, high capacity deep neural network. The MURA, a large dataset of musculoskeletal radiographs containing 40,561 images from 14,863 studies, where each study is manually labeled by radiologists as either normal or abnormal, is used in this study. We developed a Fine-Tuned model of the basic DenseNet169 model that was pre-trained on ImageNet, and compared the results of the accuracy to the base model and the modified model. In the end, we got an accuracy of almost 85% which is remarkable and can be useful and time-saving in a real-world situation.

Seasonal Color Classification

By: Alona Kalenkovich

The Four Season color classifcation system was created in 1980s in order to propose an method for selection of the most gattering colors for a person, according to his\hers colors of eyes\hair\skin\lips. In this project I explore whether there is a matematical justifcation for the four seasons classifcation system. And if such exists, is it possible to perform the work of the stylist by a computer program instead. Various deep learning models are used to approach this problem. The data for this project is obtained from a stylist who took pictures of her clients, all used pictures are publicly available. I managed to get 0.82 accuracy in prediction of the color type, and clearly images of people can be divided by this criteria.

Colorization of grayscale images

By: Tal Ladijinsky and Lior Danon

In this paper, we present our approach in making a neural network which colorizes black and white(gray-scale) images from a data-set of scene pictures which was converted from RGB to gray-scale in the data collection process. We do so by utilizing a U-Net architecture, which has shown great results for tasks of image segmentation and image colorization, and achieve mean squared error of 331.0627 after 9 epochs of training on an NVIDIA Tesla K80 GPU which proves that U-net architecture is a very suitable architecture for image colorization and specifically for the case of scene colorization. We have reduced the time and resources needed to color images by showing a lightweight architecture that produces considerable results without human intervention in the process.

Image Completion with DCGAN

By: Dan Levy and Shay Malkin

This work aims to effectively solve the problem of image inpainting. Our goal is to inpaint an image of a human face that has missing areas of pixels in a way that looks natural to the human eye. Many methods have been used for solving this problem, the best of which is by using a trained Generative Adversarial Network to produce an image that resembles the corrupted image and use it to fill in the missing parts. In this work, we’ve built and trained our own Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Network and used it, with proper techniques to inpaint images.
